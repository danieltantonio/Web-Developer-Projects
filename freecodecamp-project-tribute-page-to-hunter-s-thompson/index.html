Hunter S. Thompson's Timeline:

  • 1937: Hunter Stockton Thompson is born at Louisville, Kentucky and is the first son of three of his mother, Virginia Ray Davison and his father, Jack Robert Thompson.
  • 1952: At age 14, Hunter's father passes away at age 58. Virginia became a librarian in order to support her family and became a heavy drinker in the result of Jack's death. Hunter attended Louisville Male HighSchool, and was accepted into the Athenaneum Literary Club.
  • 1955: Hunter was rejected on his High School's yearbook project, because of his legal problems. He was seen inside of a stolen vehicle with the main culprit. He was then sent to jail for 31 days and a week. During his stay he signed up for the AirForce and wasn't allowed to do his graduation test, and therefore didn't pass HighSchool.
  • 1956: After switching multiple AirForce bases he finally stayed in Eglin AirForce Base in Florida, and even attended Florida State University. He even got a job as an Editor for the Eglin FootBall Team. He would travel with the team documenting their games. He wouldn't use his name because the AirForce wouldn't allow their soldiers to have another job.
  • 1957: Hunter was discharged from the AirForce. His Commanding Officer even stated he would not listen to authority. After this incident he worked for TIME Magazine for $51/week. He would buy a Type Writer to imitate F. Scott Fitzgerald and Ernest Hemingway.
  • 1959: He gets fired from TIME for insubordination. Hunter then would work at The Middle Town Daily. He would later get fired for destroying the office candy machine and arguing with a local business that advertises for the company.
  • 1960: Moved to Puerto Rico to work for a company called El Sportivo. After the fall of El Sportivo, Hunter worked at the New York Herald Tribune, and a few smaller papers.
  • 1961: After his return to the States. Hunter walked along U.S. Highway 40, and ended up in Big Sur, working as a Security Guard and Caretaker for Slates Hot Springs. While working for this hot springs, he would publish his first magazine called ROGUE that talked about the unlikable culture of Big Sur. This would upset the locals of Big Sur. He would later be let go at his job. During this time, he would write a few children's novels that would later be published in 1998.
  • 1962-63: He would work for a Dow Jones owned news paper called National Newspaper. Hunter then met his first wife Sandra Dawn Conklin. They would then have a son named Juan.
  • 1964: The family relocated to Glen Ellen, California. Thompson wrote for National Observer to investigate the suicide of Ernest Hemingway. He would be caught stealing from the scene.
  • 1965: The family moved to San Francisco. Hunter got involved in the drug and hippie scene, and then ended up working for Berkley Underground Paper. After that time, he would then work for The Nation, to write about the biker gang, Hell's Angels. He would live and ride with the gang, writing down their life style. Thompson would title the book, Hell's Angels: The Strange and Terrible Saga of the Outlaw Motorcycle Gangs. He would demand for a share of the profits, but then would vionelty get "stomped" by the gang.
  • Late 1960s: Thanks to the success of Hell's Angels, he was given the opportunity to write in multiple well established magazines. In TIME Magazine, he would have an article "The Hashbury is the Capital of Hippies", which is an in depth look at Hippie Culture in San Francisco.
  • 1967: The family would move back to Colorado.
  • 1968: Thompson would refuse to pay writer's taxes in order to protest the Vietname War. He would then run for president, which he would not win.
  • 1969: Hunter would receive a $15,000 royalty check, for his work with Hell's Angels.
  • 1970: Hunter ran for Sheriff of Orange County, Calorado and was stating the decriminalization of drugs. He would also say it is for personal use only and non-trafficing reasons (he didn't believe in profiteering). He was also the founder of the Freak Power Movement.
  • 1972: During a job to write about a dirtbike competition in Las Vegas. Thompson was using that time to write about "The American Dream". This book would be called Fear and Loathing In Las Vegas, a drug filled, countercultural book about Hunter's adventure in Las Vegas. Hunter would adopt a rebelious persona, speeding on the road while on drugs and acting bitter towards almost everybody. He would use this book to introduce his Gonzo style narrorative to the world. It would later become a mainstream success, and eventually turn into a movie.
  • 1981: Hunter divorced Sandra.
  • Later Years: After his success with Fear and Loathing in Las Vegas, he became more like his rebelious persona in his books. He eventually wrote more "Fear and Loathing" books about different subjects and places, but each book was almost similar to the ones before and thus became a bit repetitive. He would travel to different places like Hawaii, and work multiple jobs, and eventually settled back to writing about sports. He would also marry a new woman named Anita Bejmuk.
  • Feb 2005: Hunter was back at home in Colorado with now grown son. Feeling depressed and washed up, he put a bullet into his gun, and called his wife. He put the gun up to his head and asked Anita to come home to help him with a new ESPN article. As soon as he hung up he fired the gun. They would then find a piece of paper in his typewriter that only says: "counselor". Johnny Depp (a close friend to Thompson) held a $3 Million funeral for him.
